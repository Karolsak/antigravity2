⚡ DC Motor Chopper Drive — Complete Engineering Analysis

230 V | 960 rpm | 200 A | Ra = 0.02 Ω | Separately Excited | Chopper Drive

Problem Statement

A 230 V, 960 rpm, 200 A separately excited DC motor has an armature resistance of Ra = 0.02 Ω. The motor is fed from a chopper which provides both motoring and braking operation. The source voltage is Vs = 230 V. Assuming continuous current conduction:

  • (i) Calculate duty ratio for motoring operation at rated torque and 350 rpm.
  • (ii) Calculate duty ratio for braking operation at rated torque and 350 rpm.

Equivalent circuit (per phase, motoring): Vs → Chopper → Va → Ra → E_b (KVL: Va = E_b + I_a × R_a)

For braking (regenerative, Q2 chopper): E_b → Ra → (1−δ)·Vs (KVL: E_b = (1−δ)·Vs + I_a·Ra reversed)

Step 1 — Back EMF at Rated Conditions

E_rated = V_rated − I_a_rated × R_a
E_rated = 230 − 200 × 0.02 = 230 − 4
✅ E_rated = 226 V

Step 2 — Motor Constant K·φ

ω_rated = 2π × 960/60 = 100.53 rad/s
Kφ = E_rated / ω_rated = 226 / 100.53
✅ Kφ = 2.248 V·s/rad (= 0.2353 V·min/rev)

Step 3 — Back EMF at 350 rpm

ω_350 = 2π × 350/60 = 36.65 rad/s
E_350 = Kφ × ω_350 = 2.248 × 36.65
✅ E_350 = 82.37 V

Step 4 — I·Ra Drop at Rated Torque

ΔV = I_a × R_a = 200 × 0.02
✅ ΔV = 4 V (constant at rated torque)

Torque ∝ I_a for separately excited motor (constant flux)

(i) Motoring — Step-down (Class A) Chopper

Average motor terminal voltage: Va = δ × Vs

KVL (motor equation): Va = E_350 + I_a × Ra

δ × 230 = 82.37 + 4 = 86.37
δ = 86.37 / 230
✅ δmotoring = 0.3755  ≈ 37.55%

Interpretation: The chopper switch conducts 37.55% of each period. Since 350 rpm is well below rated (960 rpm), only ≈38% of the supply voltage is applied on average.

(ii) Braking — Regenerative (Class B) Chopper

Motor acts as generator; averaged terminal voltage: Va = (1−δ)·Vs

KVL: E_350 = Va + I_a × Ra → Va = E_350 − I_a·Ra

(1−δ) × 230 = 82.37 − 4 = 78.37
(1−δ) = 78.37 / 230 = 0.3407 → δ = 1 − 0.3407
✅ δbraking = 0.6593  ≈ 65.93%

Interpretation: The braking chopper switch conducts 65.93% of each period. The higher duty cycle keeps the current path through the armature longer, extracting regenerative energy back to the supply.

Detailed Physical Explanation

🔁 How the Motoring Chopper Works

A Class A (step-down / buck) DC-DC converter applies a pulsed voltage to the motor armature. With a switching frequency typically 1–20 kHz and continuous armature inductance current, the average voltage is Va = δ·Vs. The armature inductance L_a smooths the ripple current.

At low speeds (350 rpm ≪ 960 rpm), a small duty ratio (≈38%) suffices because the back-EMF is proportionally low. The motor then accelerates or holds 350 rpm while delivering full rated torque (200 A armature current).

♻️ How the Regenerative Braking Chopper Works

In Class B (regenerative braking) operation, the motor still rotates at 350 rpm producing E_350 = 82.37 V, but the braking torque reverses the armature current direction. A second switch (Q2) and diode are used. When Q2 is ON, current builds in the armature circuit. When Q2 is OFF, the current freewheels through D1 into the supply, returning energy.

Average terminal voltage during OFF time = Vs, so (1−δ)·Vs = 78.37 V, giving δ ≈ 66%. The regenerative energy saved goes back to the supply bus, improving overall efficiency.
